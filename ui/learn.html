How Flight Tracking Works

FlightsAboveMe shows aircraft overhead using public broadcast signals and aviation data feeds. This page explains what you’re seeing, where it comes from, and why flight data can differ between trackers.

ADS-B in a nutshell

ADS-B (Automatic Dependent Surveillance–Broadcast) lets an aircraft broadcast its position, altitude, speed, and identification. It’s “dependent” because it typically relies on onboard GPS, and it’s “broadcast” because it transmits regularly for receivers to pick up.

Most airliners and many general aviation aircraft transmit ADS-B, but what you can see depends on receiver coverage and aircraft equipment.

ADS-B vs Mode S vs MLAT

  • Mode S: Transponder messages used for surveillance/identification (not always full GPS position).
  • ADS-B: Position broadcasts (commonly GPS-based) plus identity and other telemetry.
  • MLAT (multilateration): Position estimated by comparing arrival times at multiple receivers.

Two flight tracking sites can show slightly different tracks depending on whether they’re using ADS-B position, MLAT estimates, different receiver networks, or different smoothing.

What is ICAO24?

ICAO24 is a unique six-character hexadecimal identifier associated with an aircraft’s transponder. Think of it as a digital identifier used to match live broadcasts to aircraft details.

Callsign vs flight number

The callsign is an operational identifier used in surveillance systems and radio communications. It often resembles a flight number (like DAL123), but it can also be a charter/cargo identifier or other format.

Because of that, the scheduled “flight number” you see on airline sites isn’t always a perfect match to the live callsign.

Altitude, speed, and direction

  • Altitude: Often based on barometric pressure (“baro altitude”), not exact GPS height.
  • Speed: Usually ground speed (wind affects this).
  • Direction: Typically the aircraft’s track over the ground.

Small differences in rounding, latency, or altitude reference can make trackers disagree.

Climbing, descending, and cruising

FlightsAboveMe determines climb state using vertical speed (feet per minute):

  • Positive vertical speed → Climbing
  • Negative vertical speed → Descending
  • Near zero or missing → Cruising / Level

Vertical speed can be noisy or missing in some feeds; “Cruising” can also mean “no reliable vertical speed available.”

Where FlightsAboveMe data comes from

FlightsAboveMe blends multiple sources to improve reliability and fill in aircraft details:

  • OpenSky Network — primary live state vectors when available
  • adsb.lol — fallback live aircraft state data
  • AeroDataBox — enrichment (aircraft type/model/operator details)

Coverage and update rates vary by region and source, so you may occasionally see delays or missing fields.

Data limitations

  • Flight data may be delayed, incomplete, or inaccurate.
  • Coverage depends on aircraft equipment and receiver density.
  • This site is for informational purposes only — not for navigation or operational decisions.
