How altitude is calculated
Barometric vs GPS, MSL vs AGL, and why trackers can differ

Most altitudes shown on FlightsAboveMe come from the aircraft’s transponder as barometric (pressure‑based) altitude. Air traffic control uses a standard reference pressure (29.92 inHg / 1013.25 hPa) so aircraft are all using the same baseline even when local weather pressure changes.

Barometric vs GPS altitude

Barometric altitude is not the same as GPS height. GPS altitude can vary with satellite geometry and models, while barometric altitude varies with pressure and temperature. It’s normal for different sources to disagree by a few hundred feet.

MSL vs AGL

Trackers typically display altitude above mean sea level (MSL), not height above the ground (AGL). A plane at 3,000 ft MSL over a mountain may be much closer to the terrain than it looks.

Why you may see rounded values

Depending on the data feed and message type, altitude may appear rounded (for example 25–100 ft increments). This is normal and not a sign of bad data.

Pressure altitude, flight levels, and the “29.92” rule

Above a certain altitude (called the transition altitude), pilots set a standard pressure reference (29.92 inHg / 1013.25 hPa) and altitudes are expressed as flight levels (for example FL350 = 35,000 ft). This keeps every aircraft on the same vertical reference even when local weather pressure changes.

MSL vs AGL (why a “low” altitude can still be high)

Flight tracking altitude is almost always MSL (mean sea level). If you live at 1,200 ft elevation and an aircraft shows 5,000 ft, it may be only about 3,800 ft above the ground nearby. Terrain can change quickly, so AGL is not always derivable from transponder data alone.

Why your tracker might show “jumps”

Many transponders report altitude in steps (often 25 ft or 100 ft increments depending on message type and decoding). Combine that with message timing and receiver coverage and you can see small “stair‑step” changes during climbs/descents.

Why altitude can disagree across data sources

Different networks may prefer barometric altitude, GPS altitude, or a filtered/averaged value. Some aircraft broadcast both. FlightsAboveMe prioritizes the most common, interoperable values — but small differences are normal.
